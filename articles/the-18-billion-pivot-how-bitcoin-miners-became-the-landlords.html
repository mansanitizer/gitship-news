The $18 Billion Pivot: How Bitcoin Miners Became the Landlords of AI

The infrastructure is real. The hash rate was just the opening act.

The Hardware Bureau · Feb 17, 2026

The infrastructure is real. The hash rate was just the opening act.

Here's the number that changes everything: $8.7 billion. That's what Core Scientific locked in with CoreWeave for 500 megawatts of AI infrastructure over 12 years. Add TeraWulf's $9.5 billion, 25-year joint venture with Fluidstack—backed by a $1.3 billion Google guarantee—and you're looking at nearly $20 billion in contracted revenue from companies that, two years ago, were dismissed as "crypto miners."

Your power contract is now worth more than your ASIC fleet.

The Players

Core Scientific emerged from bankruptcy to become the blueprint. Their CoreWeave deal—$8.7 billion over 12 years for 500 MW—represents the largest single AI infrastructure contract signed by any former Bitcoin miner. The math is brutal and beautiful: that's roughly $17.4 million per megawatt over the contract life, compared to Bitcoin mining's volatile $2-5 million per MW annually depending on hashprice. TeraWulf is executing at scale. In 10 months, they contracted 510 MW of critical IT load. Their October 2025 Fluidstack joint venture in Abernathy, Texas adds 168 MW with a $9.5 billion revenue commitment over 25 years. Google backstopped $1.3 billion of the lease obligations, effectively converting TeraWulf's project debt into Big Tech credit risk. CleanSpark is building the pipeline. After hiring Jeffrey Thomas—who built Saudi Arabia's multi-billion-dollar Humain AI program—the company locked up 890+ MW in the Houston area, approaching the psychologically significant 1 gigawatt threshold. No mega-lease announced yet, but the land and power are secured. The infrastructure is real.

The Arbitrage

Why are they winning? Because while VCs threw billions at AI startups, the Bitcoin miners quietly built the actual rails.

The average data center takes 5-7 years to permit and energize. CleanSpark scaled a 100 MW facility in six months. Core Scientific had 1.2 GW of contracted power sitting there, waiting. TeraWulf's Lake Mariner site offers 89% zero-carbon energy—a requirement for ESG-conscious hyperscalers that legacy data centers struggle to match.

The "Google Backstop" changed the game. When TeraWulf signs with Fluidstack, Google guarantees the lease payments. This transforms miner credit risk into investment-grade paper, unlocking project financing at 85% loan-to-cost ratios from JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs. The miners aren't just renting space anymore—they're operating a credit-enhanced infrastructure platform.

The Implication

Compute scarcity just became power scarcity. NVIDIA Blackwell is sold out through mid-2026. The bottleneck isn't GPUs—it's megawatts.

Bitcoin miners spent a decade learning to hunt stranded power: flared gas in North Dakota, hydro in Upstate New York, wind in West Texas. That expertise now feeds the AI beast. As Meltem Demirors of Crucible Capital put it: "They have the powered shell, they're ripping out the [mining machines], and their tenant is bringing the GPUs."

The sector raised over $43 billion in AI and HPC contracts in 18 months, per CoinShares. Cipher Mining's $5.5 billion AWS lease. Hut 8's $7 billion Fluidstack deal. The CoinShares Bitcoin Mining ETF soared 160% in 2025—not because Bitcoin pumped, but because the market finally understood: these aren't crypto companies anymore. They're power arbitrageurs with data center expertise.

The Kicker

The halving didn't kill Bitcoin mining. It revealed what the infrastructure was actually worth.

When the block reward dropped to 3.125 BTC in April 2024, hashprice cratered. Marginal miners died. The survivors looked at their power contracts—0.08/kWh in Texas, hydro rates in NYISO Zone A—and realized they were sitting on the scarcest asset in AI: energized, permitted, transmission-connected land.

Core Scientific's stock tripled. TeraWulf jumped 134% year-to-date. The market is pricing these companies not on their Bitcoin holdings, but on their megawatt pipelines.

The hash was just the proof of work. The power was always the point.
