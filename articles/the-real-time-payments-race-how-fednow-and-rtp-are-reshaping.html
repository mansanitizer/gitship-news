The Real-Time Payments Race: How FedNow and RTP Are Reshaping American Finance in 2025

The Fintech Correspondent · Feb 17, 2026

The United States is experiencing its most significant payments transformation in half a century. Two years after the Federal Reserve launched FedNow, the country's first new payments rail since the 1970s, the battle for instant payment dominance has intensified between this government-backed system and The Clearing House's RTP Network, which has been operational since 2017. What started as a technical infrastructure debate has evolved into a strategic inflection point for banks, credit unions, and fintechs across the nation.

The Numbers Tell a Story of Accelerating Adoption

The growth statistics are striking. FedNow processed 2.1 million payments in Q2 2025—a 62% quarterly increase—with an average daily value of $2.7 billion, up more than 400% year-over-year. The network has surpassed 1,500 participating financial institutions, reaching approximately 40% of U.S. demand deposit accounts.

Meanwhile, RTP reported processing 1.18 million payments daily in Q2 2025, with a total daily value of $481 billion—a staggering 195% leap in value from the previous quarter. RTP's network reaches roughly 71% of demand deposit accounts, giving it broader coverage despite FedNow's rapid expansion.

The divergence in participant profiles is notable: over 95% of FedNow participants are small and midsize institutions—community banks and credit unions—while RTP has historically attracted larger banks. This bifurcation reflects a fundamental tension in American banking: smaller institutions wanted instant payment capabilities without relying on infrastructure controlled by their largest competitors.

The Governance Paradox

The coexistence of two incompatible instant payment networks has created what Cornell professor Dan Awrey calls a governance challenge: "If I tried to imagine a system that was designed not to work, not from a technological perspective but from a governance perspective, we're pretty close to it."

Federal Reserve Governor Christopher Waller has offered a different framing, describing FedNow as "a solution to a large market failure"—the coordination problem of getting 8,900 banks to build their own system. Yet the practical reality remains: because the two networks don't interconnect, users on either system face limited reach. A bank using only FedNow cannot send instant payments to RTP-only recipients, and vice versa.

Use Cases Driving Real Demand

Beyond the infrastructure politics, real-world applications are emerging. FedNow's Q1 2025 data reveals key transaction types including off-cycle payroll, earned wage access, digital wallet defunding, real estate escrow payments, auto loan disbursements, and marketplace seller payouts.

The earned wage access (EWA) market exemplifies this shift. Research shows that 83% of workers prefer daily access to wages rather than waiting for scheduled paydays, and 77% opt for instant payouts when available. As EWA faces regulatory uncertainty—with some states classifying it as lending—instant payment rails are becoming the infrastructure of choice for flexible payroll solutions.

The Fraud Myth

A persistent concern holding back adoption is the fear that "faster payments equal faster fraud." Bernadette Ksepka, deputy head of product development for FedNow, calls this a misconception. "In reality, our customers have reported seeing very little fraud on FedNow to date. The irony here is that our slowest payment rail—which is check—is the one that experiences the most fraud right now."

FedNow has responded with built-in safeguards including configurable velocity thresholds and pilot programs for network intelligence tools that provide senders with risk information about recipients. These features represent a recognition that security cannot be an afterthought in instant payment infrastructure.

The Competitive Imperative

Despite growth, approximately 8,100 banks and credit unions—representing 85% of U.S. financial institutions—have yet to adopt any instant payment solution. Many that have implemented capabilities remain in "receive-only" mode, cautious about enabling outbound payments.

This hesitation carries risk. Galileo's research indicates that more than two-thirds of U.S. companies plan to join either RTP or FedNow within the next two years. The window for proactive adoption is narrowing, and institutions that delay may find themselves at a competitive disadvantage as customer expectations shift.

As Ksepka notes: "Your average American doesn't know FedNow by name. What they know is that they want to send and receive their money safely and securely, and they want to make payments at the speed they need. And they're going to do that either through their financial institution, or they're going to do that through a non-bank provider if that financial institution doesn't provide it."

Looking Ahead

The Faster Payments Council projects that instant payments will constitute 16% of the global payments mix by 2027, rising to 22% by 2028. Between 70% and 80% of financial institutions are expected to have real-time payment reception capabilities by 2028.

For American finance, 2025 represents a pivotal moment. The question is no longer whether instant payments will become standard—but which institutions will lead the transition and which will be forced to follow. The infrastructure exists. The demand is real. The only variable remaining is adoption speed.
